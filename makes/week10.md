# Week 10 – AI and Labor


The Hidden Labor of AI: Content Moderation
Overview: What Is Content Moderation?
Underneath every “clean” social media site or AI-powered chatbot, there is always a
shadowy workforce that exists, called content moderators. These are real human beings, largely
contracted employees who hail from the Global South, whose job is to filter out disturbing and
disturbingly violent content so that those living in more affluent nations do not have to encounter
such visuals.
Core question: Who does the invisible work of AI, and who profits from it?

Research Notes:
Sources Used
This research utilized primarily these investigative journalism articles and labor
accountability studies as follows by date: 2022-2026. The most critical source, which features
the offshoring of psychological risks to low-wage earners by AI companies, was the article of the
investigation by the author, Perrigo, in 2023, published in the Time magazine titled OpenAI is
exploiting thousands of Kenyan data workers who make under two dollars an hour. Yet one more
source was the one released by Tech Transparency Project in 2022 reporting wage information
and psychological results of content moderation workers, who are employed on big platforms.
Additional sources are an Amnesty International study on digital labor in 2023, which elucidated
how content moderation was applied in the context of the global AI extraction. The Guardian and
Rest of World 2024 and 2025 news articles on the trends in platform labor were also counted as
additional sources.
What Was Hardest to Find
It was challenging to find up-to-date information about wages paid to contractors on a
country-by-country basis. In a usual scenario, big tech businesses conceal their supply chains
involved in the hiring of content moderators through nondisclosure agreements and a variety of
under-contracting arrangements. Still existing information mostly relies on whistle blowing by
anonymous persons or document leaks by reporters. Documents that were updated in 2022 and
later were simpler to get compared to older documents; nonetheless, there was a substantial
discrepancy between wage estimates across the sources.
What Was Surprising
Most shocking is the fact that there is a large disparity between the profitability of the
platforms and the compensation that is offered to the employees, who work under the platforms.
These include big companies raking in millions of dollars in adverts and who need workers in
their operations that are in countries like Kenya and Philippines who earn an average of
1.50-3.00 hours. The discovery of the extent to which the wellness schemes implemented
internally are poor was also very startling.
Design Statement
Why This Format?
2
The digital zine was intentionally selected as a medium of choice as opposed to a regular
report or an infographic. Zines have some authority, some sort of counter-cultural power and this
appeared appropriate with the information on the workers who have their labor made invisible by
the companies that take advantage of it. Basically, the zine resembles the subject matter of the
subject itself; it is free, feedable and not intended to appear refined.
Audience
The target audience comprises mostly young people who are active on social media and
use all kinds of digital products, unaware of the processes happening behind the scenes. The zine
assumes that the readers do not have any knowledge of digital labor economics. Rather, it
employs simple language, figures, and color to bring the viewer in.
Intended Effect
The target is to become discomfit and become empowered. The reader has to be brought
to the feeling of being implicated, but not by being made to feel guilty and rendered incapable of
doing anything, but by making him wonder, joining the initiatives of fair-pay activism groups
and putting the websites he visits on the hot seat. Labor is a political performance that can be
seen, and in this zine, Hicks demonstrates that zines are microcosms of political performance.
