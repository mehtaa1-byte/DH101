Research Documentation
The project has its foundation on recent academic research exploring the
environmental effects of artificial intelligence. Research indicates that AI systems
consume a lot of energy in terms of computational needs, data storage, and cooling
systems. Indicatively, AI development and deployment also add to the carbon emissions
in the world, with the energy-intensive data centers (Wang et al., 2024). The other
significant discovery is that both the training and usage (inference) phases of AI are
sources of emissions. Although training large models has a large one-time carbon cost,
the use of the model in everyday life also leads to an accumulating number of emissions
over time (Tomlinson et al., 2024).
The second big challenge of this research was the inability to find steady and
credible data. The environmental cost of an AI system is not wholly disclosed by many
companies, which is why it is not possible to estimate precise emissions. Moreover, the
estimates have differences based on the sources of energy, efficiency of the server, and
the size of the models. Uncertainties also exist about the comparison of AI with human
activities. Other studies indicate that AI can prove to be more efficient than humans in
some tasks, whereas others point out its increasingly high energy requirements. This

gives a complicated image in which AI has the ability to decrease and to raise
emissions depending on the use of AI.

I use AI tools frequently for studying, research, and completing assignments.
Although each use seems small, repeated use contributes to carbon emissions. I am
willing to reduce unnecessary queries and avoid overusing AI tools for simple tasks.
However, I cannot completely stop using AI because it improves my learning efficiency

Artist Statement
The environmental cost that concerns me most is the hidden and often invisible
nature of AI’s carbon footprint. Unlike pollution from factories or vehicles, AI emissions
occur behind the scenes in data centers, making them easy to overlook. This invisibility
creates a disconnect between everyday technology use and its environmental
consequences. I believe AI is not inherently unsustainable, but its current growth raises
serious environmental concerns. Research shows that AI systems require significant
energy, especially during training and large-scale deployment (Wang et al., 2024). At
the same time, some studies suggest AI can be more efficient than human processes in
certain tasks (Tomlinson et al., 2024). This creates a tension between AI’s benefits and
its environmental costs.
For AI to become sustainable, major changes are needed. These include using
renewable energy for data centers, improving efficiency in model design, and increasing
transparency about environmental impacts. Without these changes, AI could
significantly contribute to climate change. As a user, my responsibility is to be mindful of
how I use AI. While I cannot eliminate my usage entirely, I can reduce unnecessary
5
interactions and think critically about when AI is truly needed. However, real change
requires action from governments and technology companies, as they have the greatest
influence over AI’s environmental impact.
