# Week 9 – Games and Play

The Generator
In this project, I selected Track A and a chatbot as my text generator. I had the chatbot
generate a ridiculous weather report at a time. I provided the chatbot with a set of rules. Every
output needed to sound like an official forecast, had a surreal weather condition, had a weird
public alert, and had a possible cause. I also requested a tone that was funny, poetic, and a little
bit disturbing.
The theme of my subject was ridiculous weather alerts. I chose this concept as there is
already an established structure in weather predictions. They are formal, direct and social. That
framework allowed experimenting with how foreign information can be introduced into a
familiar pattern easily. The outputs remained understandable, yet the content became strange and
unpredictable.
I created a number of outputs and stored them in a document. Out of those, I picked the
strongest ones into my selected collection. I have critiqued them in terms of clarity, surprise,
imagery, and their ability to mix official-sounding language with weird details.

Process Documentation
I created this generator by writing a single detailed prompt to Gemini. The prompt
requested the chatbot to generate one ridiculous weather report at a time in two to four sentences.
I had specific guidelines so that every output was of the same pattern. They needed each forecast
to sound official, to have one surreal weather condition, to have one public advisory, and to have
one possible cause. I also requested a tone that would be funny, poetic, and somewhat disturbing.
This arrangement provided the generator with constraints, which assisted the outputs to remain
focused.
I selected ridiculous weather reports since there is already a set style in weather language.
Projections tend to be apolitical and open. They provide terms, threats, and short descriptions.
The construction was such that it was simple to observe the difference when the content was
bizarre. The chatbot retained the form of a prediction, although the information shifted to the
world of the surreal and unpredictable.

I produced 11 outputs altogether. I placed each one of them in a document, followed by
reading them once more in order to identify patterns. A few of the outputs later, I realized that
there was a repetition in structure. The majority of them were in the same rhythm. This was
followed by a weird weather event, a public warning, and a weird cause that concluded the
report. Such a repetition made me comprehend the assignment prompt partially. The machine
was able to continue generating new combinations, but the novelty began to wane as soon as the
pattern became apparent.
I have selected the final outputs on four grounds. I sought vivid visuals, powerful diction,
comprehensible format, and a proper ratio of formal language and ridiculousness. I was not
satisfied with outputs that seemed too cluttered, too random, or too similar to previous ones.
Production of text was not the primary task. The reading, comparison, and selection of what was
worth retaining was the main work.
Artist Statement
This project altered my thoughts about creativity in generative work. Prior to this, I
assumed that creativity was largely about writing every word by yourself. Having completed this
assignment, I find the setup, the boundaries, and the decisions made after it to be creative. The
language was generated by the chatbot, and I defined the conditions. I chose the subject matter,
the structure, the regulations, and the style. I also chose the outputs that were good enough to
reflect the project.
The assignment poses the question of the position of the human in the generative work,
and in my case, the human role was the most apparent in the selection. The generator had eleven
predictions. Not every single one of them felt strong. Others were more pictorially well. Others

were more rhythmic. Others remained clearer as they got older. My role was to observe such

I also began to view the issue of the everlasting generation. Such a system may continue
generating new combinations, but the increase in output does not necessarily imply an increase
in the quality of output. The structure became known after some time. The novelty became tired.
Judgment was the significant aspect at that point. I needed to choose what was still new and what
had already begun to show some repetition.
I do not believe that AI eliminates human creativity. I believe it alters the labor.
Creativity in this project resided in the prompt, the boundaries, the reading, and the curation. The
machine provided me with a choice. I provided the project with direction and meaning. The latter
remained the most important to me.
